Using coal as a fuel for a rocket ship to Mars and back presents numerous technical challenges and significant environmental concerns. Let’s explore the feasibility by considering propulsion methods, energy requirements, and the environmental impact involved.

### Propulsion Methods

**Traditional Rocket Fuels:**
- Conventional rockets use chemical fuels like liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen due to their high energy density and efficiency.
- Chemical reactions between these compounds offer a high thrust-to-weight ratio essential for escaping Earth's gravitational pull and optimizing travel in space.

**Coal as a Rocket Fuel:**
- Coal burns by reacting with oxygen, releasing energy primarily in the form of heat, which is not effectively captured or directed in a traditional rocket propulsion context.
- The combustion of coal also generates significant amounts of carbon dioxide and other pollutants, which is counterproductive in space where minimizing mass and optimizing energy per unit mass is crucial.

**Feasibility with Current Propulsion Technologies:**
- Adapting coal for use as a rocket fuel would require a propulsion system far different from existing models, as no current technology efficiently transforms the heat and by-products of coal combustion into adequate thrust for space travel.
- Moving away from liquid or solid rocket propellants would mean re-designing propulsion systems, which would be inefficient compared to current standards that focus on maximizing specific impulse and minimizing weight.

### Energy Requirements

- Rockets to Mars need an extraordinary amount of energy to escape Earth’s atmosphere, navigate space, and eventually land and return from Mars.
- The sheer mass and volume required to carry enough coal for a round trip would vastly exceed practical limits.
- Coal’s energy density (in MJ/kg) is significantly lower than that of conventional rocket fuels, meaning more mass would need to be carried, complicating spacecraft design and increasing launch costs significantly.

### Environmental Impact

- **On Earth:**
  - Mining, transporting, and processing coal have considerable environmental impacts, contributing to deforestation, habitat destruction, and pollution.
  - Coal combustion is a significant source of greenhouse gases and other pollutants, which is against current trends of reducing emissions, especially in high-visibility initiatives like space exploration.

- **In Space:**
  - Imagine the illogicality of managing and disposing of coal combustion by-products in space, which could pose hazards to the spacecraft and equipment.
  - Concerns would exist about planetary protection protocols, especially minimizing contamination of Mars by Earth materials, which coal combustion would heavily violate.

### Conclusion

Using coal as a fuel for rockets to Mars is impractical with current technology and understanding of propulsion systems. It fails to match the necessary energy efficiency, weight constraints, and environmental responsibility needed for space travel. Instead, continued advancements in chemical, nuclear thermal, or electric propulsion systems offer more promising, efficient, and cleaner alternatives for future interplanetary travel. The focus remains on minimizing environmental impact while maximizing energy efficiency and spacecraft capability, goals that coal cannot feasibly meet.
